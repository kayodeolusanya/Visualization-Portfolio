Grocery shelves emptied, prices climbed, and millions struggled to afford basic meals. In the years that followed, many governments and food companies turned to Artificial Intelligence as a solution hoping smarter systems could prevent another crisis. AI began predicting crop yields with greater accuracy, automating warehouses, optimizing transport routes, and reducing food waste. In some regions, these tools helped stabilize prices and improve access. Yet in others, the benefits were uneven. Small farmers without access to technology fell behind, and efficiency gains did not always translate into lower consumer prices. Using COVID-19 as a benchmark, the story reveals a mixed reality: AI has the power to make food more affordable and resilient but only if its benefits are shared widely. The pandemic taught the world how fragile food systems can be; the future will depend on how responsibly AI is woven into them.
